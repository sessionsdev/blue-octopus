Willis T. Pettijohn


393rd Regiment 99th Infantry Division

May 9, 1920 - March 28, 1945




Willis T. Pettijohn (Terrell) was my great-grandfather who served, was captured, and later killed in action near Hummelburg, Germany during World War II.

Below is a letter to his wife from a fellow POW describing the circumstances of his death during an attempted escape. And a letter from Willis to his brother describing his time in Camp Blanding,Florida and his hopes to become a farmer when he returns home.

The Letter from Captain Crocker to Mrs. Pettijohn

Addressed to:

Mrs. Dena Pettijohn
3330 E. Rosedale
Fort Worth, Tex.


From:

Captain John S. Crocker
0524827
Ward 20C
Lawson General Hospital
Chamblee, Ga.


Dear Mrs. Pettijohn,

Your letter reached me recently after several forwardings. I believe I can give you some of the information you desire.


When the tanks and other vehicles left camp to return to our lines a number of us rode the outside of the vehicles. Lt. Pettijohn drew the lead tank. A few miles from the camp, the column ran into a German roadblock. The first shot of a German bazooka struck the lead tank, killing four persons, your husband included. The others were 2 POW officers and an enlisted man from the rear of the column. It was pitch dark at the time, and I did not know where we were.


After several other occurrences I was recaptured with a few others and taken to a small town called Gillbeck (it is on the road from Hammelburg to Gemunden, about 7 kilometers south of Hammelburg). It is little more than a crossroads. The German in charge told me that there were four Americans there who had been killed in the fight the evening before. Then I realized that we had returned to the roadblock.


I took several enlisted men, borrowed some shovels, and went to bury them. I was able to identify them all positively. By the nature of their wounds I am certain all died instantly.


We buried them near the junction of a little creek and the highway, making each grave with a cross with the name written in indelible pencil. The graves are very noticeable from the highway and will undoubtedly be found by units organized for that purpose. Nothing was removed from the bodies because I felt it would be more valuable on the body as an aid in identification.


As soon as I had the chance I turned over full details to two other officers with instructions to report the matter if they survived. I made a full report at Le Havre and later at the placement of the matter.


Lt. Pettijohn and I lived in the same room at Hammelburg for about 3 months. He was one of the definitely good people there and I grew to be very fond of him. We often spoke of you and looked forward eagerly to being reunited with you.


If your surmise that the date of your husband's death was on 28 March is correct, it was very early in the morning.


I wish I could say something to comfort you in your loss but words are inadequate. Please feel free to call on me at any time for any information you wish that I might have.


Yours truly,
John S. Crocker
Capt. 29th

Letter from Willis T. Pettijohn to His Brother

Camp Blanding, Fla
June 16, 1942


Dear Strip & Family,

I suppose that you have decided that I didn't intend to write to you. I'm sorry, but I have just kept putting it off. I have lost your address, & I am just hoping that you get this.


I suppose you have heard of this beautiful Florida — well, it isn't what it is cracked up to be. I suppose that it is ok, but I had much rather be in Texas. I haven't been around much down here to see if I like it — It cost too much to go anyplace. They are really after these soldier's dollars. It is very few of mine that they get I can tell you.


How are all of you? How is Patricia Ann? Gosh, I would really like to see her. Mother sent me a picture of her, & I think she looks like me — Don't you? She sure is sweet. I suppose that Joyce & Bud are about grown. I would like to have a snapshot of them if you have one. I know they must have grown a lot, but I can't imagine what they look like. Joyce will be 16 in August — will Glen be 8 or 9 in December?


This camp down here sure is a big one — there is 168 sections in it and about a 100,000 men. Everywhere you go there are a thousand soldiers. I will be glad when this is all over. I don't think we will ever go across — sometimes I wonder if I'm going to get to be over there. Hitler is my meat — the Japs don't amount to much. I believe the war will be over by next spring — then I will be out of the army by the fall following that. As soon as I get out I'm going to start farming — I think I will have enough money. I'm going to do my best anyway.


So long for now — Write to me & I promise that I won't wait so long to write next time.


Answer a brother,
Terrell

Rites Set for Lt. Pettijohn

First-Lieutenant W. Terrell Pettijohn, who was captured Dec. 17, 1944 in the Battle of the Bulge in Belgium and was killed in action March 28, 1945, will be brought to Stephenville Wednesday, Dec. 15, where funeral services will be held in the First Methodist Church at 3 p.m.


Rev. William H. Cole, a former pastor here, will officiate, and interment will be made in the American Legion Field of Honor in the East End Cemetery with full military honors to be conferred by the Turnbow-Higgs Post No. 240. Music will be furnished by the First Methodist Church.


The body, which had been interred in Holland, was among those returned to the States aboard the ship Carrol Victory Nov. 15, later being moved to the Quartermaster Depot in Ft. Worth. It was brought to Stephenville by the Trewitt Funeral Home, which has charge of arrangements, with an official army officer escort. The body will remain at the funeral home until time for the services.


Was Football Star


Terrell Pettijohn, who was a football star while attending the local high school, joined Co. D, 142nd Infantry, 36th Division, the local unit of the Texas National Guard, in 1941, and moved to Brownwood, when it mobilized.


He went to Florida and Massachusetts with the unit, and while the division was in Massachusetts, awaiting orders to go overseas, he was assigned to the officers' training school at Ft. Benning, Ga., where he received his commission.


He was stationed at Camp Wolters, Mineral Wells, and in April 1943, while there, was married to Dena Clark, daughter of Wilbur Clark of Stamford, formerly of Erath County. Her father and Henry Clark of Stephenville are brothers.

Rites Held for Lieut. Pettijohn

Lt. Willis Terrell Pettijohn was reburied in the American Legion Field of Honor in the East End Cemetery, following services held Wednesday afternoon, Dec. 15, at 3 o'clock in the local First Methodist Church.


Rev. R. L. Graham, of Coleman, officiated at the rites, assisted by Rev. Joseph Jones, Stephenville. The Turnbow-Higgs American Legion Post No. 240 conferred the military honors.


Pallbearers were John Deane Jr., Clinton Cox, R. N. Pittman, Paul Carr, Zane Carter, Bras Cole, Gordon Hill, Elbert Pollan, and Olin Cameron.


Lieutenant Pettijohn was born May 9, 1920 in Comanche County. He was the son of the late Mr. Robert Lee Pettijohn and Mrs. Elizabeth Salter Pettijohn. In November 1940 he entered military service and April 16, 1943, while stationed at Camp Wolters, Mineral Wells, he was married to Dena Clark in Stamford.


Killed in Germany


After going overseas, he was captured Dec. 17, 1944, in the Battle of the Bulge in Belgium. He was a prisoner in Oflag 13-B and, March 28, 1945, he was killed as he was being liberated from that camp, which was located in Hammelburg, Germany.


He had been a member of the Methodist Church since just a young boy.


Surviving him are his widow, Mrs. Dena Pettijohn, and a daughter, Dena Sue, Arlington; his mother, Mrs. R. L. Pettijohn, Stephenville; a sister, Mrs. J. C. Roulston, Stephenville; and four brothers, Harry Pettijohn of Stephenville, D. S. and J. C. Pettijohn of Plainview and Robert Pettijohn of Gorman.


Trewitt Funeral Home had charge of arrangements.

Captured by Nazis

Pettijohn was sent to Camp Maxey, Paris, and in September 1944 he left for overseas duty, going immediately to the front.


On Dec. 18, 1944, in Belgium, Lieutenant Pettijohn was taken prisoner by the Germans during the Battle of the Bulge. He was in two or more camps until March 28, 1945, when he was liberated from Hammelburg, Germany. It was a short time after his release on a road south of the town, somewhere between Hammelburg and Gemunden, that the Americans ran into a road blockade. The first shot from a German bazooka struck the lead tank and killed Lieutenant Pettijohn and three others, according to an account received by Mrs. Terrell Pettijohn from one of the lieutenant's companions who escaped.


Lieutenant Pettijohn served with the 393rd Infantry, 99th Division, First Army, and had an outstanding record as a combat officer.


Besides the widow, Mrs. Dena Clark Pettijohn, Arlington, the lieutenant is survived by a daughter, Sue, four and a half years old; his mother, Mrs. Lizzie Pettijohn, West Frey Street, Stephenville; a sister, Mrs. J. C. Roulston, Stephenville; four brothers, Harry of California, J. Colbert and D. Striplin of Plainview and Bob of Gorman; and numerous uncles, aunts and cousins of Dublin, Comanche and Gustine.
